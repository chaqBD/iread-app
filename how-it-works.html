The Process

How iRead Works

Each month follows a clear democratic process — from nomination to live discussion and beyond. Here is everything from the four-step cycle to the 13-dimension review framework.

Monthly Cycle

The four-step reading cycle

Each month follows a clear democratic process — from nomination to live discussion and beyond.

01
📚

Nominate

Members nominate one article each via Google Form. Must include full citation, ABS ranking, and a 2-sentence rationale.

02
🗳️

Vote

A curated shortlist of 5 goes to community vote via WhatsApp and LinkedIn polls. Top 3 articles are selected.

03
🔍

Critique

Members dissect the article — interrogating central claims, challenging evidence, and surfacing counter-arguments. A podcast episode drops on the last Thursday to prime the discussion.

04
💬

Debate

Last Thursday of the month: a 90-minute live virtual session. Reviewers present. The community debates claims and counter-claims. Gaps are mapped.

Session Format

90 minutes of pure intellectual engagement

Critique Framework

The iRead Critique Framework

Every article dissected through the same critical lens. We interrogate claims, expose weaknesses, and map what the research leaves unanswered.

01
Central Claim
What is the paper's core argument? Can you state it in one sentence — and does the paper actually prove it?
02
Evidence Quality
How credible and sufficient is the evidence? Is the data robust enough to support the claim?
03
Counter-Claims
What challenges or contradicts the central argument? What would a sceptic say?
04
Methodological Critique
Where does the research design fail? Sampling bias, analytical flaws, missing controls?
05
Theoretical Tension
Does this sit in genuine tension with existing theory, or does it merely replicate it with new data?
06
Gaps & Blind Spots
What did the authors ignore, overlook, or deliberately avoid? What remains unanswered?
07
Real-World Validity
Does this hold beyond the study context? Can the findings be generalised to the UK or global settings?
08
Community Verdict
Score 1–10. Cite, extend, or reject? Is the central claim defensible? Group consensus vs. dissent — all documented.
